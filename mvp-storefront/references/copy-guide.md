# Storefront Copy Guide

How to write landing page copy that converts. Connects the template sections to the frameworks in the wiki (~/wiki). Read this before filling in placeholders.

## Before You Write: Diagnose the Awareness Level

Every section of copy depends on where the visitor is mentally. The landing page template assumes **Problem Aware** by default (cold traffic from ads). If you know the traffic is warmer, adjust accordingly.

**Quick diagnostic:**
- Cold traffic from broad ads → Problem Aware (they feel the pain, don't know solutions exist)
- Search traffic for specific terms → Solution Aware (they're looking for a fix)
- Retargeting / email click → Product/Most Aware (they know you, need the deal)

**Why this matters:** Problem Aware copy leads with the pain. Solution Aware copy leads with the mechanism. Product Aware copy leads with proof. Writing the wrong one is the #1 conversion killer.

See: `~/wiki/concepts/schwartz-awareness-levels.md` for the full framework.

## Section-by-Section Guide

### Hero (Headline + Subhead)

**The job:** Stop the scroll. Make them think "this is for me."

**Headline principles:**
- Address the pain or the outcome, not the product
- Specific beats vague: "3 hours back every Monday" beats "Save time"
- The headline is not a description of what the product IS — it's what the product DOES for them
- If you can't write a good headline, you don't understand the pain point well enough

**Awareness-level adjustments:**
- Problem Aware (default): Name the pain. "You're spending 4 hours on something that should take 20 minutes."
- Solution Aware: Name the approach. "The invoicing tool that writes itself from your calendar."
- Product Aware: Name the result. "423 freelancers stopped losing track of payments. Here's how."

**Subhead:** One sentence that bridges pain to promise. Not a feature list. Not "all-in-one solution." Just: here's what changes.

Bad: "A powerful, easy-to-use productivity platform for modern teams."
Good: "Stop losing 3 hours a week to work you've already done."

### Problem Section (Pain Cards)

**The job:** Make the visitor feel understood. "This product was built by someone who gets it."

**Three pain points, three different angles on the same core frustration:**
1. The daily annoyance (what happens every day)
2. The hidden cost (what it's actually costing — time, money, opportunity)
3. The emotional toll (frustration, dread, feeling stupid for not fixing it)

**Write as direct statements, not fake testimonials.** "Manual invoicing takes 4 hours a week" — not "I used to spend 4 hours a week on invoicing" (that's a fake quote and visitors sense it).

**Connection to Cialdini:** This section builds Liking (they feel understood) and sets up Reciprocity (you've articulated their problem better than they could — now they trust your solution).

See: `~/wiki/concepts/cialdini-principles-of-persuasion.md`

### Solution Section (Benefits)

**The job:** Show what life looks like after. Outcomes, not features.

**Three benefits, each answering "what changes for me?":**
- Benefit titles: 2-4 words naming the outcome. "Reclaim your Mondays" not "Automated reporting"
- Benefit descriptions: One sentence. Specific. "Your weekly report assembles itself from data that's already there — you review and send."

**The Hormozi Value Equation lens:**
Each benefit should reduce one of the four drag variables:
- Time Delay -> "Done in 2 minutes, not 2 hours"
- Effort -> "No setup, no categories, it just works"
- Likelihood of Achievement -> "Works with the tools you already use"
- Dream Outcome -> "Close your laptop at 5 knowing everything's handled"

See: `~/wiki/concepts/hormozi-value-equation.md`

**Common mistake:** Writing features as benefits. "Drag-and-drop interface" is a feature. "Build your report in 2 minutes without touching a spreadsheet" is a benefit.

### Proof Section (Stats)

**The job:** Reduce skepticism. "This actually works."

**Three stats — and they MUST be verifiable.** No fabricated user counts. No fake testimonials. Options:
- Technical specs: "Processes 10,000 rows in under 3 seconds"
- Time savings: "Average setup time: 8 minutes"
- Guarantee: "$0 setup fee" or "30-day money back"
- Industry data: "83% of freelancers invoice late (Freelancers Union, 2024)"
- Product specs: "Works offline. No account required."

**Why verifiable matters:** One fake stat poisons the entire page. Visitors who catch it won't trust anything else. The ALLMIND brand is built on honesty — the copy should reflect that.

**Connection to Cialdini:** This is Authority (specs demonstrate competence) and Social Proof (industry data shows others face this problem).

### FAQ Section

**The job:** Remove the top 3 objections without being defensive.

**The three questions to answer:**
1. The trust objection: "Does this actually work?" / "What if it doesn't work for me?"
2. The friction objection: "How much setup is involved?" / "Do I need technical skills?"
3. The value objection: "Why should I pay for this when [free alternative] exists?"

**Answer principles:**
- Be direct. Don't hedge. "Yes, it works. Here's exactly why."
- Acknowledge the objection's validity. "Fair question. Most free tools do X, but they don't Y."
- Keep answers to 1-2 sentences. This is not the place for paragraphs.

**The guarantee is your best FAQ answer.** If you offer a money-back guarantee, mention it here AND in the FAQ. It answers "what if it doesn't work?" without you having to argue.

### CTA (Closing)

**The job:** Convert the scrollers who read the whole page. Restate the value differently than the hero.

**CTA Headline:** Different words, same promise. If the hero said "Stop losing hours," the CTA might say "Your time back starts now."

**CTA Subtext:** Gentle urgency or risk reversal. "One-time purchase. No subscription. Yours forever." — this is both urgency (act now) and risk reversal (no ongoing obligation).

**CTA Button text:** Action verb + context. "Get Access — $7" is better than "Buy Now" (what am I buying?) and better than "Get Started" (started with what?).

**Connection to pricing psychology:** The price in the button is an anchor. "$7" in the CTA makes the page feel like a low-commitment purchase. "$47" in the CTA requires the page to have built more value first.

See: `~/wiki/concepts/pricing-psychology-and-strategy.md`

## Cross-Cutting Principles

### Specificity Converts
"Save time" -> weak.
"Save 3 hours a week" -> strong.
"Save 3 hours a week on invoices" -> strongest.

Every time you write a vague claim, ask: "Can I put a number or concrete detail here?" If yes, do it.

### One Idea Per Sentence
If a sentence has "and" connecting two different ideas, split it. Visitors scan — they don't read. Each sentence should deliver one punch.

### Write Like a Human
Read your copy out loud. If it sounds like marketing, rewrite it. The best copy sounds like a smart friend explaining something they're excited about, not a corporation delivering a value proposition.

### No Fake Anything
- No fake testimonials
- No fake user counts
- No fake urgency ("Only 3 left!" when there are unlimited)
- No fake scarcity ("Offer expires tonight!" when it doesn't)

Fake elements destroy trust. Trust is the only thing that converts cold visitors into buyers. ONE caught fake and the brand is damaged permanently.

### The Awareness-Level Test
After writing all copy, read the whole page and ask: "What awareness level does this assume?"
- If the headline names a pain -> Problem Aware
- If the headline names a mechanism -> Solution Aware
- If the headline names a result -> Product Aware

Make sure EVERY section matches the same level. A Problem Aware headline followed by a Product Aware solution section creates cognitive dissonance.
