# Copy Angle Library

Proven angles organized by awareness level. These are starting points for thinking, not templates to fill in. The LLM should use these as inspiration for how to approach each awareness level, then write custom copy for the specific product.

## Problem Aware Angles

The prospect feels pain but doesn't know solutions exist. Lead with the problem.

### The "Name Their Day" Angle
Describe their exact daily frustration in vivid detail. Make them think "how do you know my life?"
> "Every Monday you open the spreadsheet. Again. The one with 47 tabs. The one that breaks if you breathe on it wrong. You spend 2 hours updating data that should take 10 minutes..."

### The "Hidden Cost" Angle
Reveal a cost they haven't calculated. Make the invisible visible.
> "You're not just losing 4 hours a week to manual invoicing. You're losing the deals you could have closed in those 4 hours. At your average deal size, that's $X/month you're leaving on the table."

### The "It Doesn't Have To Be This Way" Angle
Validate their frustration, then plant the seed that a solution exists.
> "Spending 3 hours on a task that should take 20 minutes isn't a 'you' problem. It's a tools problem. And it's fixable."

### The "What You Tried Already" Angle
Acknowledge what they've already attempted and why it didn't work.
> "You've tried the spreadsheet. You've tried the free tools. You've tried just 'being more organized.' They didn't work because they're solving the wrong problem."

## Solution Aware Angles

The prospect knows solutions exist but doesn't know your product. Lead with your unique approach.

### The "We Tried Everything Too" Angle
Position yourself as someone who had the same problem and built the solution from experience.
> "We spent 6 months trying every project management tool on the market. They were all either too complex or too simple. So we built exactly what we needed. Turns out everyone else needed it too."

### The "The Real Problem Is..." Angle
Reframe the problem. They think it's X, but it's actually Y. Your product solves Y.
> "You don't need another to-do list. You need something that tells you what NOT to do. That's what we built."

### The "Mechanism" Angle
Explain HOW your product works differently. The mechanism is the differentiator.
> "Other tools ask you to categorize everything manually. Ours watches how you actually work for 3 days, then organizes itself around your patterns. No setup. No categories. It just learns."

### The "Comparison Without Comparing" Angle
Position against the category without naming competitors.
> "Most [category] tools were built for enterprise teams with dedicated admins. This was built for the person who IS the team."

## Product Aware Angles

The prospect knows your product but isn't convinced. Lead with proof and differentiation.

### The "Numbers" Angle
Hard results from real users. Specificity is credibility.
> "423 users. Average time to first result: 8 minutes. Average time saved per week: 4.2 hours. 91% still using it after 90 days."

### The "Before/After" Angle
Show the transformation with concrete detail.
> "Before: 6 hours on Monday updating reports. After: 12 minutes, automated. Here's exactly what changed."

### The "Objection Killer" Angle
Address the #1 reason they haven't bought yet, directly.
> "You're thinking: 'I've tried tools like this before.' Fair. Here's what's different — and here's what happens if it doesn't work for you (nothing, it's free to try)."

### The "Risk Reversal" Angle
Make the guarantee the headline. Remove all risk.
> "Use it for 30 days. If it doesn't save you at least 3 hours a week, delete it and keep the templates. No questions. No awkward emails."

## Unaware Angles

The prospect doesn't recognize the problem. You can't sell yet — just create awareness.

### The "Did You Know" Angle
Factual revelation that creates awareness.
> "The average knowledge worker spends 2.5 hours per day searching for information they already have. Not learning new things. Finding stuff they already made."

### The "Story" Angle
Tell someone else's story. Let them see themselves in it.
> "Sarah was managing 12 clients with Gmail labels and sticky notes. It worked until it didn't. One missed deadline cost her the biggest account of her career..."

### The "Contrarian Take" Angle
Challenge an assumption they hold.
> "Productivity tools don't make you productive. They make you busy. Here's the difference..."

---

## Universal Angles (Work at Multiple Levels)

### The "Specific Mechanism + Guarantee" Combo
Explain how it works AND guarantee the result. Double credibility.
> "[Mechanism description]. That's why we guarantee [specific outcome] in [timeframe] or your money back. No questions."

### The "For [Specific Person]" Angle
Narrow your audience in the headline itself. Specificity = relevance.
> "For freelancers managing 5+ clients: stop losing track of what's owed."

### The "Negative Space" Angle
Define what it's NOT. Contrast with category baggage.
> "Not another project management tool. Not another spreadsheet. Not another 'productivity hack.' Just [one specific thing], done right."

---

## How to Use This Library

1. Determine the prospect's awareness level (Step 2 of main workflow)
2. Pick 2-3 angles that fit the product and awareness level
3. Write custom copy for each angle — the angle guides the APPROACH, not the words
4. Tag each piece with its angle for the testing matrix
5. If none of these angles fit, invent a new one — these are patterns, not boundaries
